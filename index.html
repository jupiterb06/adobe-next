a (kind of) complete overview of the creative scene at cornell university |

as explained by jupiter bhanot |

mouse over the different nodes and connections to learn about (some of) the different departments, organizations, and spaces. nodes with this icon indicate that i’m involved in them.

of course not! cornell university’s creative scene is huge. this map focuses on organizations with their roots in visual arts and design, but we have hundreds of musical and performance groups, literary magazines, project teams, craft groups, and everything in-between. one of the biggest things that defines our community is collaboration-- organizations are always co-sponsoring events and promoting one another. it’s common for students who are engaged creatively, such as myself, to participate in multiple organizations at once. people are also super passionate and intrinsically driven-- when Cornell students want something done, it doesn’t just “get done”, it gets produced.

one of the things that i believe is holding back Cornell's creative community is also, ironically, one of our greatest strengths. Cornell has a huge culture of ambition, and while that pushes students to do a lot of really amazing work, it also creates an intrinsic fear of failure or, perhaps even worse, “wasting time”. while some of our creative organizations are more laid back, many have recruitment processes that range from lengthy applications to three or four rounds of interviews. students who don’t see immediate, personal success in these organizations often struggle to remain invested, especially if they’re not also pursuing a creative field on an academic or professional level. as a leader in several of these organizations, i’ve heard many of my peers say that they’ve left organizations after one semester because they weren’t able to get a position on the executive board. i get where they’re coming from-- our academics are tough, and when factoring in jobs, project teams, and other opportunities, it can be difficult to justify fitting in activities that don’t directly advance professional goals. it’s a stereotype on campus that Cornell students create calendar events for everything from house parties to self-care tasks to dates, and that “locked in” culture prevents people from experimenting. this fear of experimentation and this need to move at a rapid pace also keeps people from pushing deeper and understanding the “why” of design-- everything is product focused, rather than process.

my vision for Adobe on Cornell campus is to leverage the interconnectedness of our scene and create spaces where creativity isn’t tied to recruitment cycles or resume building. the first thing i’d do is host a series of high-energy, low-pressure “design nights”. each event would be led in collaboration with a single organization, department or professor, and would focus on their specific philosophy, method, or medium of design. while i’d like these events to be grounded in skill-building and hands-on experimentation, i think having an element of theory in the workshops would be a great way to encourage students to think critically about design, and to understand what draws them to specific approaches, styles, and disciplines, as well as expose them to new ones.

eventually, i’d like to host design nights with as many different groups as possible, but i’d start off by hosting one with the art department. obviously, i’m a little biased, but the art department offers the most comprehensive adobe coursework at Cornell, and their approach to the creative process is one rooted in research and experimentation, which is exactly what i’m trying to foster. one of my secondary goals as a leader for adobe on campus is to promote and uplift initiatives that are already in place-- the art department, despite all the amazing work it produces and the interdisciplinary courses it offers, is borderline unheard of in the wider campus community, and i think events like design nights would be the perfect way to highlight the department and other less well-known organizations.

i believe i’m the best person to represent adobe on campus because i’m someone who loves to get people together. my experience as a leader in creative student organizations has taught me how to cross-collaborate, how to get people from different disciplines to get on the same page and synthesize their unique skills, perspectives, and interests to create a cohesive-yet-dynamic final product. i work with incoming art students to help them not only adjust to being college students, but to being full-time creatives, to help them find what inspires them at Cornell and balance making work for their classes and work for themselves. outside of my leadership roles, i’m always planning events for my friends, whether it’s a movie night in my dorm or an after-hours dance party in the drawing studio.

i love to try new things. as an art major at cornell, my course requirements are evenly split between “major” courses-- studios, art history, theory and criticism --and out of department electives. i use these electives as a way to explore fields like computer science, game design, literature, communications, and media studies, and to bring what i learn into my creative practice. as a designer, i’m really influenced by technology. i grew up working for my dad’s IT business, which inspired an early interest in the physical side of computing. our house was always filled with old and obsolete hardware, and those aesthetics became a huge part of my artistic practice. at the same time, i also grew up in silicon valley, where technology changes and mutates at a pace that’s near impossible to keep up with. what’s interesting to me is where the old and new meet-- even the latest and greatest, most state-of-the-art hardware is dependent on software and frameworks that are considered obsolete, and that juxtaposition is what really drives my view on emerging technologies like AI.

i’m really excited about the ways that creatives have been engaging with AI on a conceptual level. artificial intelligence opens up a lot of interesting questions regarding language, originality, and the ever-shifting relationship between creativity and technological advancement. designers and artists who use AI in their work in a way that explore these questions are, in my opinion, the ones making the best use of the technology. i also think it’s interesting to use AI as a tool for challenging your existing perspectives; seeing how an LLM interprets my ideas has helped me recontextualize them and encouraged me to think more deeply about what i’m saying with my work. at the same time, though, i think my concerns reflect a lot of the broader anxieties regarding AI; namely, the fear that AI will be used to replace human creatives, and that those who remain will be forced into depending on it. i think AI as a tool is no different than any other, with its own specific applications, and that, when it is used well, enhances the creative process of experimentation and revision, rather than replaces it. i want to see more AI tools made by and for creatives, tools that accurately interpret our needs and that seek to work with us, rather than simply approximate what we do.
